## AHTS-Specific Requirements

Anchor Handling Tug Supply vessels operate at the demanding intersection of heavy towing and offshore anchor deployment — a combination that places exceptional requirements on towing equipment that standard tug-grade systems are not designed to meet.

The fundamental difference is **dual-medium handling**. An AHTS must manage both tow wire (typically 76–152 mm diameter high-tensile steel wire) and offshore mooring chain (usually R3 or R4 grade, 76–162 mm diameter stud-link) in the course of a single operation. The shark jaw system must accommodate both without re-rigging the aft deck arrangement.

Beyond dual-medium capability, AHTS operations demand:

- **High SWL at sustained load** — anchor handling can impose static chain loads far above the vessel's nominal bollard pull; SWL ratings of 200–700 MT must be maintained under shock loading conditions typical of chain catenary drop
- **Precise load control** — anchor deployment sequences require the operator to pay out or hold chain at defined tensions; integration between the shark jaw and the anchor handling winch control system is essential
- **Rapid emergency release** — chain or wire must be releasable under full load within the time specified by the relevant offshore classification notation (typically under 10 seconds for DNV AHTS class)
- **Deck wash and corrosion resistance** — the AHTS stern working deck is continuously exposed to seawater, mud returns, and hydraulic fluid. Jaw bodies, pivot pins, and actuator housings require stainless or high-grade duplex material in critical interfaces

## Operational Scenarios

### Anchor Deployment and Retrieval

In anchor handling, the vessel backs down to a pre-positioned buoy or pennant wire, picks up the anchor chain leader, and pays out chain into a pre-engineered mooring pattern under controlled tension. The shark jaw holds the chain leader at the vessel stern while the AHTS positions; on release command, chain is paid out at controlled speed.

During retrieval, the shark jaw must accept chain links under load as the winch pulls the anchor free of the seabed — a sequence that can generate shock loads 2–3× the static chain tension. Hydraulic circuit accumulator sizing must account for this dynamic factor.

### Rig Towing

When moving a semi-submersible or jack-up rig between locations, the AHTS serves as the primary or secondary towing vessel. Tow wire diameters for rig towing typically exceed 90 mm, and towing durations may run 5–15 days in open ocean. The shark jaw jaw throat and wear surfaces must be designed for sustained grip without fretting damage to the wire.

For rig tow operations in areas subject to SOLAS inspection, the shark jaw release system must be certified operable from the bridge; most DNV-class AHTS vessels require a third release point from the emergency station as well.

### Emergency Operations

Emergency towing (ETV) operations can require connection of a tow to a disabled vessel in Sea State 5 or higher. In these conditions, the ability to safely connect and hold a tow wire or chain without crew working over the stern roller is critical. Remote-operable shark jaw systems have been a significant factor in reducing stern deck fatalities in emergency towing incidents over the past two decades.

## Classification Requirements for AHTS

### DNV Offshore Class Notation

For vessels intended for North Sea or other DNV-regulated offshore operations, the shark jaw must comply with DNV's **AHTS** class notation, which specifies:

- Type testing of the complete jaw assembly at 1.25× SWL static proof load
- Fatigue testing documentation for the primary structural members (typically 10⁶ cycles)
- Hydraulic system redundancy — dual-circuit or accumulator backup to maintain jaw closure in the event of HPU failure
- Remote release system type approval with documented response time at maximum SWL

### OCIMF Compliance

The Oil Companies International Marine Forum (OCIMF) issues guidelines for anchor handling operations that many major oil company contracts reference. Key OCIMF requirements relevant to shark jaw specification include:

- Minimum jaw holding capacity stated as a percentage of AHTS bollard pull (OCIMF typically references 1.1–1.25× bollard pull as minimum jaw SWL)
- Documented chain size range the jaw can handle without modification
- Emergency release mechanism accessible without tools and operable by a single person

### ABS and BV Considerations

ABS Offshore Service Vessel (OSV) notation and Bureau Veritas AHT notation both require type-approval documentation for the shark jaw assembly and third-party witnessed proof load testing. All SharkJaw systems supplied for ABS or BV-classed vessels are delivered with the relevant Society's type approval certificate and installation inspection records.

## HPU Requirements for AHTS

### Power and Flow Rate

AHTS hydraulic power requirements are substantially higher than for tug applications due to the combination of higher SWL ratings and the need to operate the shark jaw simultaneously with the anchor handling winch hydraulic circuit. A dedicated HPU for the shark jaw system is strongly preferred over drawing from the vessel's common hydraulic ring main.

Typical HPU specifications for AHTS shark jaw service:

- **Working pressure**: 280–350 bar (dependent on jaw actuator bore size and SWL)
- **Flow rate**: 150–400 L/min continuous, sized to achieve rated jaw closure speed within 5 seconds
- **Accumulator volume**: minimum 50 L pre-charged, sized to maintain full jaw closure force for 30 seconds without HPU power (DNV requirement for some notations)
- **Power input**: 37–132 kW electric motor or dedicated engine-driven pump, depending on vessel electrical budget

### Redundancy Architecture

For AHTS vessels operating under OCIMF-referenced contracts or DNV AHTS notation, single-failure redundancy in the HPU is required. This is typically achieved by either:

- **Dual-pump configuration** — two independently driven pumps on a common manifold; either pump can sustain full jaw closure force
- **Accumulator standby** — a primary HPU supported by a fully charged high-capacity accumulator bank, sized to complete a full emergency release cycle plus maintain closure for the required hold time

The HPU control cabinet must be rated to at least IP56 for AHTS stern deck installation, with all hydraulic connections using swaged hose assemblies or SAE-flanged rigid pipe (no threaded fittings in the high-pressure circuit).
